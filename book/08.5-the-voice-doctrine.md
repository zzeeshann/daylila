# 08.5 — The voice doctrine

Most software has a coding style guide. Zeemish has a writing standard. The standard is what stops every piece from sounding like every other AI-generated thing on the internet — calm, balanced, explanatory, a little bit syllabus, a little bit LinkedIn. That tone is the default the model lands on if you let it. The doctrine is the thing that pulls it somewhere else.

This chapter explains what that thing is, where it lives in the code, and why it is structured the way it is.

## Two layers

The voice has two layers, and they do different jobs.

The **operational contract** is the polish layer. It is a list. No tribe words. Plain English. Short sentences. No flattery. 1000–1500 words across all beats. The rules are mechanical — you can spot a violation by ctrl-F. The contract was the original voice system, and for the first few months it was enough.

The **doctrine** is the deeper layer. It does not list. It teaches. It names a posture: drop the reader into something already happening, find the specific person, let contradictions stand without resolving them, end on an image not a moral, never write *"this matters because"*. The doctrine is long-form prose. The Drafter reads it before writing each piece, the way a journalist might re-read a memo from their editor before sitting down at the keyboard.

The doctrine is the bar. The contract is the polish. If they conflict, the doctrine wins.

## Why two layers

Because the contract alone wasn't enough.

A piece called *"How Cartel Gold Becomes American"* shipped on 2026-04-26 with a voice score of 95 — the contract said it was clean. No tribe words. Short sentences. Specific examples. Plain English. By every mechanical rule the piece was correct. And it still read like a report being read into a microphone. The hook previewed the lesson. The close summarised. Contradictions resolved into tidy morals. *"This matters because"* connective tissue did the reader's thinking for them.

The contract could not see the failure because the failure was not mechanical. It was postural. A list of rules cannot describe the difference between *"a person who has understood something telling another person what they found"* and *"a writer producing prose that ticks every box"*.

That difference is what the doctrine names. And once the doctrine was added — once the Drafter was instructed to read it before writing, and the Voice Auditor was instructed to judge against it — the next piece looked different. Specific people instead of categories. Contradictions held. The close was shorter. Not perfect, but visibly different.

## Why Manto

The doctrine's contents currently express the standard through Saadat Hasan Manto's writing discipline.

Manto was a Pakistani Urdu short story writer who died in 1955 in Lahore. He wrote about partition — the splitting of British India into India and Pakistan in 1947 — and about the violence and madness and ordinariness of that splitting. He was tried for obscenity six times across two countries. His lawyers argued his stories weren't obscene, they were mirrors. The judges disagreed. He kept writing.

He is the standard because of what he refused to do. He never explained his own point. He started in the middle of something already happening and walked away. He wrote specific human beings — Bishan Singh, a Sikh man from a village called Toba Tek Singh, who could not determine after partition whether his village was now in India or Pakistan and stood on the dividing line until he died there — instead of categories like "a refugee". He let contradictions stand. He ended on images, not conclusions.

Those refusals are what Zeemish needs. Most AI writing is trained to resolve. To explain. To wrap. The doctrine's job is to teach the system to refuse those moves.

## Why the principle is bigger than Manto

The constant in the code is named `VOICE_DOCTRINE`, not `MANTO_VOICE`. The principle the doctrine encodes is not "write like Manto". It is *clear teaching, not robotic prose*.

If a future operator decides Manto isn't the right framing — that Chekhov's restraint or de Maupassant's economy or an operator-original framing teaches the standard better — the contents of the doctrine file change. The architecture stays. The Drafter still reads "the Zeemish voice doctrine" at the start of each piece. The auditor still judges against it. Only the prose inside the file is different.

That separation matters because writing standards are themselves systems that evolve. The doctrine is supposed to evolve. What the architecture protects is the discipline of having a doctrine — not the specific contents of any particular one.

## Where the doctrine lives

For the human reader: [`content/ZEEMISH_MANTO_VOICE.md`](../content/ZEEMISH_MANTO_VOICE.md). Long-form, prose, written as instruction to the Drafter.

For the agents at runtime: [`agents/src/shared/voice-doctrine.ts`](../agents/src/shared/voice-doctrine.ts). A TypeScript file that exports the doctrine as a string constant called `VOICE_DOCTRINE`. The Drafter, Voice Auditor, and Integrator import it.

The two files must stay in sync. There's no automatic mirror — it's a discipline. The .md is the canonical form for human editing. The .ts is what the agents actually load. If you change one without the other, the human-facing doc and the runtime behaviour drift. The same rule applies to the operational contract — `content/voice-contract.md` and `agents/src/shared/voice-contract.ts` are the human-facing and runtime-facing copies of the same thing.

The full file inventory, the operator's how-to-change-the-voice procedure, the verification flow, and the planned future where an admin can select between voices from a dropdown all live in [`docs/VOICE.md`](../docs/VOICE.md). That doc is for the operator. This chapter is for the reader.

## What the doctrine is not

It is not a style guide. Style guides describe how a thing already sounds. The doctrine prescribes how a thing should sound — and pulls against the model's defaults to keep it there.

It is not optional. Every piece that ships in Zeemish's name was written against the doctrine and passed an auditor that judged the piece against the doctrine. There is no fast-path that skips it.

It is not finished. Each iteration of the doctrine — and there will be more — is backed by an actual published-piece failure, not a theoretical one. The 2026-04-27 geofence piece shipped with a multi-sentence dramatic title and a four-sentence rhetorical close; the doctrine was tightened that same evening to name the new failure modes. The doctrine evolves the way the system evolves. Incrementally. Honestly. In the open.

The four words from the previous chapter — humbleness, quietness, calmness, consciousness — are the test every piece passes or fails. The doctrine is what teaches the writer how to write so the test can be passed.
