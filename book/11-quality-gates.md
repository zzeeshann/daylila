# 11 — The quality gates

*Status: outline. To be expanded by a future session — see WRITING-MORE.md.*

---

## What this chapter covers

- Why "let the AI write it and ship" doesn't work for a daily publishing system.
- The three gates in detail: Voice Auditor, Fact Checker, Structure Editor.
- What each one checks, what its pass/fail thresholds are, and why those thresholds.
- The Integrator pattern: take the feedback, revise, resubmit. Up to three rounds.
- Tier labels on published pieces — Polished (≥85), Solid (70–84), Rough (<70) — and why all of them ship. Since 2026-05-06, the threshold values and the ship-anyway rule live in the audit contract (`content/audit-contract.md`) — pulled out of the code-side docstrings so the rules and their reasoning sit together in one place.
- What happens when a gate consistently fails on a topic: the piece is not forced through. It escalates to a human.

## Why this matters for Daylila

- The gates are what separates "autonomous publishing" from "spam engine." They are the thing that makes daily autonomous publishing safe enough to brand as a teaching system.
- Each gate is itself a Claude call with a specialised prompt. Keeping the writing call and the judging calls separate is deliberate — chapter 6 explains why.
- The Fact Checker reaches the live web through Tavily Search (replaced Anthropic's `web_search_20250305` server tool 2026-05-16, which had itself replaced DuckDuckGo Instant Answer 2026-04-30). It now runs a three-step pipeline per audit round: Claude extracts the list of verifiable claims from the draft, the system hits Tavily per claim in parallel (with a per-claim cache so the same fact looked up twice — within a piece, or across pieces — only costs one search), then a second Claude call reads the compact snippets and decides verdict per claim. The drawer surfaces each claim's status and note, plus a round-level "Sources consulted" line listing the unique domains the snippets came from — chapter 9 has the full agent description. Why the change: the previous Anthropic-tool path stuffed every search result back into Claude's context, so a piece needing three audit rounds racked up 100k+ input tokens just on the fact-check loop. The new pipeline caps each call's input around 15k by sending only curated snippets, cutting per-piece cost by ~95% on the fact-check block (~$1.13 → ~$0.05) without losing verdict quality. The per-claim cache is the other unlock: evergreen facts ("Hydrogen absorbs at 121.6 nanometers") cached on one piece satisfy any future piece's check, and the cache row stays forever — its 30-day rule is about *refreshing* dormant news facts, not deleting them.
- As of 2026-05-07, every revision the Integrator makes is recorded — what the Drafter wrote first, the prose at every round, and one row per piece of feedback with its disposition (accepted, overruled, partial) and a one-sentence reason. Two new tables hold this: `draft_revisions` for the per-round MDX, `integrator_decisions` for the per-feedback record. The published piece in git stays unchanged; D1 holds the trail. Why this matters: until now, only the final piece survived. If the Voice Auditor flagged a sentence and the Integrator rewrote it, the original prose was gone and so was the reason. The system could ship a piece without remembering what it had to fix to get there. Now the *How this was made* drawer can show the real revision trail when an operator picks up the rendering work, and the Learner gets a much richer signal about which kinds of feedback recur and which kinds tend to be overruled. The ground for the next layer of self-improvement sits underneath the loop now.
- Also as of 2026-05-07, the Integrator sees what's working — not just what's broken. Every revision round shows all three audit dimensions, marked PASS or FAIL, with an explicit instruction to preserve the passing ones. Within a single piece, the Integrator also remembers the previous round's audits, so Round 2 can see "voice was passing at 95 in Round 1" alongside Round 2's results. If a passing dimension flips to failing across rounds, the Integrator now treats that as a regression to repair — not a fresh problem to ignore. Why this matters: pieces that used to ship as Rough because the Integrator was chasing its tail across dimensions now have a real chance of landing Solid or Polished. The catalyst was a 2026-05-06 piece on magic-mushroom anatomy: voice scored 95 in Round 1, dropped to 92 in Round 2 after the Integrator introduced a banned word while fixing structure, recovered to 95 in Round 3 — two rounds wasted on a problem the Integrator could have prevented if it had known what was passing. The Foundation Fix programme closes here.
- As of 2026-05-08, the *How this was made* drawer reads as a clear narrative arc — making, audit, fix, **final state**, learn forward. A new Final-state block sits between the per-round audit notes and the rest of the metadata; it names what shipped (tier, voice score, facts and structure verdicts) so a reader doesn't carry a "this article was bad" impression into the forward-looking sections below. Below that, the reflective notes at the bottom — what the Drafter would do differently next time, patterns extracted for tomorrow's Drafter — are framed as forward-looking guidance, not as a verdict on what shipped. Why this matters: the drawer's honesty is part of the brand, but honesty without scope-framing was misleading. A piece that scored Voice 95 and passed all audits would still surface a self-reflection saying "the close lands flat", and a reader would think the system thought the article was bad. It didn't. The reflection was a note for tomorrow's Drafter. The drawer now says so.
