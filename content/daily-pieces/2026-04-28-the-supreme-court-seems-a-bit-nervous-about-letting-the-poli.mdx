## hook

Justice Kagan leans forward: "So if the police want to know where someone was Tuesday at 3pm, that's one thing. If they want to know everywhere they've been for half a year, that's… what, exactly?"

The lawyer representing Timothy Carpenter is standing before the Court explaining why police obtained 127 days of his cell location records without a warrant. Not where Carpenter was during the four armed robberies he was convicted of planning. Everywhere he was between December 2010 and March 2011. The file showed 12,898 separate location points. Which gas stations. Which girlfriend's house. Which mosque on Friday afternoons.

What does the Fourth Amendment call a search you didn't know was happening?

## teaching-1

Your phone pings a cell tower every six to eight seconds when you're moving. The tower logs the connection: phone ID, tower ID, timestamp. Three towers triangulate position within fifty to three hundred meters, depending on density. This is not GPS precision. It's network housekeeping. The phone needs the tower to route calls. The tower needs the ping to know which cell you're in. The log is a byproduct.

The carrier stores these logs. Not on your device — you can't delete them, you can't opt out. They exist because the system requires them to work. MetroPCS, Carpenter's carrier, held the data for two years. Sprint holds it for eighteen months. Verizon for one year. You have no access to this history. You didn't ask for it to be created. But it maps everywhere you carried your phone.

The data is not surveillance until someone asks for the file. Then it becomes retroactive tracking. The FBI presented MetroPCS with a court order under the Stored Communications Act — a standard lower than a warrant, requiring only "reasonable grounds to believe" the records are "relevant and material to an ongoing investigation." MetroPCS handed over the file. The FBI mapped 127 days of Carpenter's movements. Carpenter found out at trial.

## teaching-2

The Fourth Amendment says: "The right of the people to be secure in their persons, houses, papers, and effects, against unreasonable searches and seizures, shall not be violated."

The word "search" originally meant physical intrusion. A cop opening your drawer. In 1967, the Supreme Court added a second layer: reasonable expectation of privacy. If you expect privacy, and society agrees that expectation is reasonable, then violating it counts as a search — even without physical intrusion. That doctrine was built for wiretaps. A device placed on a phone line. Listening in real time. You knew when you picked up the phone that someone could be listening.

Location data collapses that structure. The tracking already happened. Your phone already pinged the towers. The log already exists. You had no expectation of privacy during the ping because you didn't know the ping was being recorded. The carrier was doing network maintenance, not surveillance. The surveillance begins when police request the file — but by then the data is historical. They are not watching you. They are reading where you were.

The log existed before the search. The search is reading the log. The Constitution doesn't have a word for that.

## teaching-3

The distinction between "where were you" and "where have you been" is not semantic. One is a point: were you at 5501 Gould Street at 4:04pm when the T-Mobile store was robbed? The other is a pattern: who do you meet, where do you sleep, which neighborhoods do you avoid, how often do you visit the mosque or the union hall or the medical clinic at the edge of town.

Police can follow you in person. They can sit outside your house, watch you leave, trail your car. The Supreme Court has held this is not a search — you are in public, you have no expectation of privacy in your movements when anyone can see you. But physical surveillance has limits. It requires officers, vehicles, shifts. It is visible if you look for it. You can change your route.

Retrospective location data removes those limits. The police learn your pattern without ever being in the same place as you. They do it silently, after the fact, from a file. Carpenter's 12,898 location points showed the FBI which RadioShack he visited four times in one week, which house he returned to every night, which cell tower his phone pinged most often between 11pm and 6am. Not just where he was during the robberies. The structure of his life.

The question is not whether police can learn where you go. They can. The question is whether they can learn your entire history of movement without a warrant, compiled from data you generated as a condition of using the network, logged by a third party you never spoke to.

## teaching-4

The government's argument at oral argument was built on a 1979 case called *Smith v. Maryland*. Police asked the phone company for a list of numbers a robbery suspect dialed. The Supreme Court said that wasn't a search. You have no expectation of privacy in information you voluntarily give to a third party. When you dial a number, you know the phone company sees it — that's how the call gets routed. You gave up privacy when you used the service.

Location data is not a number you dialed. It is a ping generated automatically, every six seconds, whether you touch the phone or not. You do not hand it to the carrier. The phone hands it over while you walk to work. The third-party doctrine was written for information you knowingly share. Location logs are information your phone shares without asking.

Carpenter's lawyer argued this to the Court. The FBI knows where you sleep, where you pray, where you go when you're not at work. They learn this from records you can't see, didn't create, and don't control. That is surveillance. The Fourth Amendment calls it a search.

The government's lawyer replied: the records belong to MetroPCS. Carpenter gave them the data when he signed up for service. He has no property interest in what the carrier chooses to log. If the carrier wants to share those logs with the FBI, that's between the carrier and the FBI. Carpenter isn't a party to it.

The argument turned on this: is location data something you share, or something that leaks from you while you move through the world?

## watch

The Sixth Circuit ruled for the government in 2016. Carpenter appealed to the Supreme Court. Oral arguments happened in November 2017. A ruling is expected by June 2018. Watch whether the warrant requirement applies only to future requests, or whether it invalidates data already collected under the old standard. Watch which states change their retention policies before the opinion lands. Watch whether the ruling distinguishes between short-term tracking (a day, a week) and long-term tracking (months). The line gets drawn somewhere. Where it gets drawn determines which files become searches.
